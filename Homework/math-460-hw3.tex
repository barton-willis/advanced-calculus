\question [10] Let $(\mathcal{F},+, \times)$ be a field with $\theta$ as its additive identity. And let $a \in \mathcal{F}_{\neq \theta}$ and $b \in \mathcal{F}$. Show that there is a unique $x \in \mathcal{F}$ such that $a \times x = b$.


\question Define $\rat$ to be the set of rational numbers. Thus $\frac{2}{3} \in \rat$ and $\sqrt{2} \notin \rat$, for example.
For $(a,b), (a^\prime, b^\prime) \in \rat \times \rat$, define the binary operators $+$ and $\times$
on $\rat \times \rat$ by
\begin{align*}
  (a,b) +  (a^\prime, b^\prime) &= (a + a^\prime,b + b^\prime), \\
  (a,b) \times  (a^\prime, b^\prime) &= (a a^\prime + 2 b b^\prime, a b^\prime + b a^\prime).
\end{align*}
These definitions make $(\rat \times \rat, +, \times)$ into a field. If you don't believe me, I invite you to check. But
you can take my word.

\begin{parts}

\part [10] Find the additive identity for this field. You don't need to show the algebra you used to find the
additive identity, but you do need to prove that your putative answer is correct.

\begin{solution} We'll show that the additive identity for this 
  field is $(0,0)$.

  \begin{proof} Let $(a,b) \in \mathcal{F}$, We have
    \begin{equation*}
       (a,b) + (0,0) = (a+0, b+0) = (a,b).
    \end{equation*}
    So $(0,0)$ is the additive identity for $\mathcal{F}$.
  \end{proof}

  
\end{solution}
\part [10] Find the multiplicative identity for this field. You don't need to show  the algebra you used to find the
multiplication identity, but you do need to prove that
your putative answer is correct.

\begin{solution}
We'll show that the multiplicative identity for this 
  field is $(1,0)$.

  \begin{proof} Let $(a,b) \in \mathcal{F}$, We have
    \begin{equation*}
       (a,b) \times (1,0) = (a \times 1 + b \times 0, a \times 0 + b \times 1)
               = (a,b).
    \end{equation*}
    So $(1,0)$ is the multiplicative  identity for $\mathcal{F}$.
  \end{proof}
\end{solution}
\part [10] Find the multiplicative inverse of $(1,2)$. You don't 
need to show the algebra you used to find the answer, but you do need to prove that your putative answer is correct.

\begin{solution} We'll show that $(1,2)^{-1} = \left( -\frac{1}{7},  \frac{2}{7} \right)$. 

\begin{proof} We have
\begin{equation*}
  (1,2) \times \left( -\frac{1}{7},  \frac{2}{7} \right) =
   \left(-\frac{1}{7} + \frac{8}{7}, \frac{2}{7}  - \frac{2}{7}\right) = (1,0).
\end{equation*}
So $(1,2)^{-1} = \left(-\frac{1}{7},  \frac{2}{7} \right)$. \qedhere
\end{proof}
\end{solution}


\part [10] For $a,b \in     \rat$, a calculation gives
\begin{equation}
   (a,b)^{-1} = \left(- \frac{a}{2 b^2 - a^2}, \frac{b}{2 b^2 - a^2}\right).
\end{equation}
But wait! What's the story if $2 b^2 - a^2 = 0$?  Either, I flubbed and $(\rat \times \rat, +, \times)$
isn't a field, or the above formula for the inverse is rubbish, or something else. What is the something else?

\begin{solution} Since $\sqrt{2} \notin \rat$, for $a,b \in \rat$, we have $2 b^2 - a^2 = 0$ iff $a=0$ and $b=0$.
Although the denominator looks like it might vanish, it doesn't vanish unless both $a=0$ and $b=0$.

\end{solution}
